Almost every conversation about Palestine and Israel starts in the wrong place. It starts in 1948, or 1967, or October 7, 2023 — depending on who's telling it. And whichever starting point you choose, the other side accuses you of omitting the context that changes everything.

They're both right. Context changes everything. So this guide starts further back, moves chronologically, and names what it sees — using the same analytical framework it would apply anywhere: who holds power, who doesn't, and where the money and weapons come from.

One thing stated clearly at the outset: this is not a conflict between two equal sides. One party has one of the most powerful militaries on earth, backed unconditionally by the world's dominant superpower. The other has been under military occupation, siege, or dispossession for 75 years. That asymmetry is the starting point, not a conclusion.

The Frame — Settler Colonialism

Israel was built as a settler-colonial project — one designed not to rule the people already on the land, but to replace them. Its own founders said so, in writing. Get this one idea and the rest of the story snaps into focus.

To understand Palestine, you need one concept: settler colonialism. It's different from regular colonialism, and the distinction matters.

Regular colonialism extracts — a foreign power takes resources, labour, and land from a population but ultimately intends to leave, or at least to maintain a native population as a workforce. The British in India needed Indians. The Belgians in the Congo needed Congolese labour.

Settler colonialism eliminates. The goal is not to exploit the existing population but to replace it — to make the land available for permanent settlement by people from elsewhere. The existing population is not a resource to be used; it is an obstacle to be removed. The most documented example in modern history is the colonisation of North America. What happened to Native populations wasn't incidental to the settlement project — it was the settlement project.

The defining logic of settler colonialism

Patrick Wolfe, the historian who formalised the concept, stated it plainly: "Settler colonialism destroys to replace." The land is the target. The people on it are in the way. This is not a description of the intent of every individual settler — it is a description of the structural logic of the project as a whole.

This frame applies directly to Zionist settlement in Palestine. The Zionist movement — founded in 1897 by Theodor Herzl in response to European antisemitism — sought to establish a Jewish homeland. Palestine was chosen as the location, partly for religious and historical reasons, but there was a fundamental problem: Palestine was not empty. It had a population of approximately 500,000 Arab Palestinians, most of whom had lived there for generations.

The early Zionist leaders were largely honest about this tension, at least in their private correspondence. Herzl's diary discusses the need to "spirit the penniless population across the border." Ze'ev Jabotinsky, founder of Revisionist Zionism (the ideological forefather of Likud, Israel's current governing party), wrote in 1923 that Palestinians would never voluntarily accept displacement and that settlement could only proceed behind an "iron wall" of military force.

This is not an accusation. It is the documented intellectual history of the movement that founded Israel. Understanding it doesn't require denying the reality of European antisemitism, the horror of the Holocaust, or the genuine need of Jewish refugees for safety. It requires holding those truths alongside the truth of what happened to the people who were already in Palestine.

Why the frame matters: If you see this as a territorial dispute between two peoples with competing claims, the appropriate response is negotiation and compromise. If you see it as a settler colonial situation — where one party is being progressively eliminated from their land by an externally-backed settler project — the analysis is different, the moral stakes are different, and the concept of "both sides" becomes incoherent in a specific way. An occupied people's resistance to occupation cannot be morally equated with the violence of the occupying power. Frantz Fanon argued this in 1961. It remains the correct analytical distinction.

The History — Ottoman Palestine to the Nakba

Palestine was never empty. Over a few decades it was turned from an Arab-majority land into a state where the majority had been expelled or scattered — 750,000 people, 500+ villages destroyed, none ever allowed home. Here it is, year by year.

Pre-1880s

Ottoman Palestine

Palestine is a province of the Ottoman Empire, home to roughly 500,000 people — predominantly Arab Muslims and Christians, with a small Jewish minority (around 5% of the population) that had lived there continuously for centuries alongside Arab neighbours. The society is agrarian, with established land ownership patterns, trade networks, and a distinct Palestinian Arab cultural identity.

1897–1917

Zionist Settlement Begins

The Zionist movement is founded with the goal of a Jewish state. Settlers begin purchasing land in Palestine, displacing Palestinian tenant farmers. By 1914, the Jewish population grows from around 5% to 12%.

1917

The Balfour Declaration

Britain promises support for a "Jewish national home" in Palestine — land it doesn't control, about a population it doesn't consult. The 500,000 Arab Palestinians are described as "existing non-Jewish communities."

1920–1947

The British Mandate

Jewish immigration rises from 60,000 to 630,000, partly driven by the Holocaust. Palestinian revolt is crushed. By 1947, settlers are 33% of the population but own about 7% of the land.

Deep Dive: The Mandate Era & Early Migration — how settlement worked before 1948

The First Zionist Congress met in Basle in 1897. Herzl founded the movement with the explicit goal of a Jewish state. Early settlers purchased land from absentee Ottoman landlords, often displacing Palestinian tenant farmers who had worked the land for generations. Tensions with Palestinian Arab residents began immediately over land, labour exclusion policies, and political organisation.

British Foreign Secretary Arthur Balfour wrote to Lord Rothschild declaring British support for "the establishment in Palestine of a national home for the Jewish people." The letter also stated this should not prejudice "the civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities in Palestine." Britain had no legal authority over Palestine at this point — it was still Ottoman territory. The promise was made by one colonial power about land it didn't own, to a third party, about a population that was not asked.

After WWI, Britain received a League of Nations mandate over Palestine. Jewish immigration accelerated — partly driven by the Holocaust and European antisemitism. The 1936–39 Arab Revolt was a general strike and armed uprising against British rule and Zionist immigration, brutally suppressed by British forces with the assistance of Zionist paramilitary organisations.

1947

The UN Partition Plan

The UN proposes splitting Palestine: 56% of the land to the Jewish minority (33% of the population), 44% to the Arab majority. The Jewish Agency accepts. Palestinians reject it — 33% of the population getting 56% of the territory, including the most productive land, without the consent of those who own most of it.

1947–1949

The Nakba — "The Catastrophe"

Over 750,000 Palestinians — more than half the Arab population — are expelled or flee. Over 500 villages are destroyed. Massacres are documented, most notably Deir Yassin (107 killed). Israel declares independence in May 1948, wins the war, expands beyond partition lines. The refugees are never permitted to return.

Deep Dive: 1948 — the Nakba in documented detail

Before Israel's declaration of independence, Zionist military forces — primarily the Haganah and its elite unit the Palmach — began military operations in areas allocated to the Jewish state and beyond. What followed is documented by Israeli historians (Benny Morris, Ilan Pappé) using Israeli military archives.

On April 9, 1948, forces from the Irgun and Lehi attacked the village of Deir Yassin near Jerusalem. Between 107 and 120 villagers were killed, including women and children. The attack was documented by the International Red Cross. The news spread across Palestine, contributing to the mass flight of Palestinians from surrounding areas.

Plan Dalet. Declassified in the 1980s, this Haganah military plan specified operations to "cleanse" areas of Arab villages, with explicit provision for destroying villages and expelling populations. Historian Ilan Pappé uses it as evidence of planned ethnic cleansing. Benny Morris disputes the characterisation while acknowledging expulsion was the result.

For decades, Israel's official position characterised Palestinian displacement as voluntary — Palestinians left because Arab leaders told them to. The opening of Israeli military archives in the 1980s produced "New Historians" — Israeli scholars who documented from internal records that the displacement was organised and in many areas explicitly ordered. The historical facts are not seriously contested among scholars.

Deep Dive — The Nakba

The Catastrophe — What Actually Happened

In under two years, around 85% of the Palestinians in what became Israel were expelled or fled, 400+ villages were bulldozed, and not one refugee was ever allowed home. Everything that followed sits on top of this.

Between November 1947 and the end of 1948, the demographic and physical landscape of Palestine was transformed in ways that permanently shaped everything that followed. Here is what the historical record — including Israeli military archives and the work of Israeli historians — shows.

The scale of displacement. Approximately 750,000 Palestinians — between 80 and 85% of the Arab population of what became Israel — left or were expelled from their homes. They fled to the West Bank, Gaza, Jordan, Lebanon, and Syria, where they and their descendants remain as refugees to this day. The UN counts approximately 5.9 million registered Palestinian refugees globally as of 2023.

The villages. Israeli historian Walid Khalidi documented 418 Palestinian villages that were depopulated during the Nakba. Of these, the majority were subsequently demolished — bulldozed, mined, or built over. Some were converted into Israeli communities or parks. The village of Deheisha became a refugee camp. The site of Lifta, on the outskirts of Jerusalem, stands partially intact and is one of the few remaining visible remnants of a pre-1948 Palestinian village.

The psychological effect — the creation of terror to accelerate departure — was understood and, in some cases, deliberately employed.

Plan Dalet. Declassified in the 1980s, Plan Dalet was a Haganah military plan adopted in March 1948 that specified operations to "cleanse" areas of Arab villages, with explicit provision for destroying villages and expelling populations that resisted. Historian Ilan Pappé uses Plan Dalet as evidence for his argument that the expulsion was planned ethnic cleansing. Benny Morris disputes the characterisation of it as a blueprint for expulsion while acknowledging that expulsion was the result. The document exists; its content is not disputed.

The right of return. UN Resolution 194, passed in December 1948, states that Palestinian refugees wishing to return to their homes and live at peace with their neighbours should be permitted to do so at the earliest practicable date. Israel has rejected this resolution consistently for 75 years. The right of return remains the central unresolved issue of the conflict — and the one whose resolution would most directly challenge the demographic composition of the Israeli state.

Why the Nakba is the foundation: Every subsequent development — the 1967 occupation, the settlement project, Gaza, October 7 — is downstream of 1948. The Palestinian refugee population exists because of the Nakba. The territorial claims exist because of the Nakba. The resistance exists because of the Nakba. You cannot understand anything that has happened since without it.

The Occupation — 1967 to the Present

Since 1967, Israel has run the longest military occupation in the modern world — seizing land, planting settlements international law calls illegal, and carving the West Bank into pieces until a Palestinian state became physically impossible. Here's the timeline.

In June 1967, Israel fought and won a six-day war against Egypt, Jordan, and Syria. It captured the West Bank, Gaza, the Sinai, and the Golan Heights. The Sinai was later returned. Everything else remains under Israeli control — for over 57 years, the longest military occupation in the modern world.

1967

The Occupation Begins

Israel captures the West Bank and Gaza. First settlements — civilian communities on occupied land — are built immediately. International law prohibits this. Israel rejects that position.

Deep Dive: 1967 — the Six-Day War & the beginning of occupation

In six days, Israel captured the West Bank (including East Jerusalem) from Jordan, the Gaza Strip and Sinai Peninsula from Egypt, and the Golan Heights from Syria. Israel established military administration over the West Bank and Gaza immediately.

The first Israeli settlements — civilian communities built on occupied Palestinian land — began in 1967, immediately after the war. The Fourth Geneva Convention prohibits an occupying power from transferring its civilian population into occupied territory. Israel has consistently rejected the applicability of this provision. The international legal consensus, including advisory opinions from the International Court of Justice, holds that the settlements are illegal.

Settlement construction accelerated under Likud governments. By 1993, over 100,000 Israeli settlers lived in the West Bank.

1987–1993

First Intifada

A Palestinian uprising — stone-throwing, strikes, civil disobedience — erupts in Gaza and spreads. Israel responds with military force. Over 1,000 Palestinians killed; 160 Israelis killed.

1993

The Oslo Accords

Oslo promises a Palestinian state within five years. Thirty years later, no state exists. Settlements more than doubled during and after the "peace process."

Deep Dive: The Oslo Accords — the gridlocked peace process

Signed on the White House lawn, the Oslo Accords created the Palestinian Authority, established the framework of a "two-state solution," and divided the West Bank into Areas A (Palestinian civil and security control), B (Palestinian civil, Israeli security), and C (full Israeli control — covering 60% of the West Bank).

Oslo was designed as a five-year transition to a Palestinian state. Settlement construction accelerated during and after Oslo — the settler population more than doubled. Critics argue Oslo functioned as Palestinian management, not liberation — requiring Palestinian leaders to police their own population on Israel's behalf while Israel continued colonising the land.

The Camp David negotiations in 2000 collapsed, and no subsequent framework has come closer to producing a Palestinian state. The Oslo framework remains the nominal basis of international diplomacy while the situation on the ground has moved steadily in the opposite direction.

2000–2005

The Second Intifada

Far more violent than the first. Palestinian factions carry out suicide bombings targeting Israeli civilians. Over 1,000 Israeli civilians killed. Israel responds with targeted assassinations, raids, and a separation wall. Over 3,000 Palestinians killed. The wall, ruled illegal by the ICJ, annexes Palestinian land.

2007–Present

The Gaza Blockade

Hamas takes control of Gaza. Israel imposes a total blockade — controlling all movement of people, goods, food, and medicine. The UN calls it collective punishment. Gaza's 2.3 million people live in what has been described as an "open-air prison." Israel has bombed Gaza four times since 2008 — in 2008–09, 2012, 2014, and 2021 — killing thousands of Palestinian civilians each time.

2023

Settlements Today

Over 700,000 Israeli settlers now live in the West Bank and East Jerusalem. The settlement project has made a contiguous Palestinian state geographically impossible without removing them. Most of the international community considers them illegal.

Deep Dive — The Settlement Project

How Settlements Work — and What They're Doing

"Settlements" sound small. Many are cities of tens of thousands, built on occupied land and linked by roads Palestinians are banned from using — a state-funded system engineered to make a Palestinian state impossible.

The word "settlement" makes them sound like small communities. In practice, many are cities. Ariel has a population of 20,000 and a university. Ma'ale Adumim, east of Jerusalem, has 40,000 residents and bisects the West Bank. They have swimming pools, shopping malls, and subsidised housing — built on land that international law considers Palestinian territory under illegal occupation.

How they expand. The process is systematic: the Israeli military declares an area "state land" or a "closed military zone." Palestinian landowners are removed or denied access. Infrastructure — roads, electricity, water — is built. Settlers move in, often incentivised by subsidised housing costs 30–40% below Israeli market rates. Over time, the settlement expands, often absorbing more surrounding land through "outposts" — small settler encampments that are technically illegal even under Israeli law, but which the Israeli military protects and which are later retroactively legalised.

The road system. To connect settlements to Israel proper, Israel has built a network of roads across the West Bank that Palestinians are prohibited from using. This "bypass road" system physically fragments Palestinian territory into disconnected enclaves. A Palestinian driving between two West Bank cities must navigate checkpoints and circuitous routes around settlement areas. The same journey for an Israeli settler takes a fraction of the time on direct roads.

What they're doing to the two-state solution. The two-state solution — a Palestinian state in the West Bank and Gaza alongside Israel — has been the nominal framework of international diplomacy since Oslo. But settlements have made it physically difficult to implement. A Palestinian state requires territorial contiguity — land that connects. Settlements, bypass roads, and the wall have carved the West Bank into fragments. They are a state policy, funded by the Israeli government, protected by the Israeli military, and — critically — continued through every Israeli government regardless of political orientation. Labor governments built settlements. Likud governments built settlements. The settlement project has continued through every peace process, every ceasefire, and every international objection. It is the actual policy, regardless of what Israeli governments say at negotiating tables.
Deep Dive — The Apartheid Question

Why Human Rights Organisations Use That Word

Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and Israel's own B'Tselem all reached the same conclusion: Israel is committing the crime of apartheid. Here's the documented basis for that word.

In 2021, Human Rights Watch — one of the world's most respected human rights organisations — published a 213-page report concluding that Israeli authorities are committing the crime of apartheid against Palestinians. Amnesty International published a similar finding in 2022. B'Tselem, Israel's leading human rights organisation, reached the same conclusion in 2021.

The word apartheid is charged, so it's worth being precise about what it means legally. Under the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, apartheid is defined as inhumane acts committed in the context of an institutionalised regime of systematic oppression and domination by one racial group over another, with the intent to maintain that regime.

Here is what the reports document:

Two separate legal systems. Israeli settlers in the West Bank live under Israeli civil law — with full civil rights, access to Israeli courts, freedom of movement, and the right to vote in Israeli elections. Palestinians living in the same territory — under Israeli military occupation — live under military law. They are tried in military courts with conviction rates above 99%. They cannot vote in Israeli elections. They require permits to move, build, or access many areas. Two populations living in the same territory under entirely different legal regimes, with rights determined by ethnicity.

Movement restrictions. Palestinians in the West Bank require permits to enter East Jerusalem, to travel through Area C, and to exit to Jordan. The permit system is administered by the Israeli military and denials are common. Gaza has been under a comprehensive siege since 2007. Palestinians cannot travel between the West Bank and Gaza — the two territories of the supposed future Palestinian state — without Israeli permission, which is rarely granted.

Land and resource control. In Area C (60% of the West Bank), Palestinian construction requires Israeli military permits — which are denied at a rate of approximately 98.5%. Israeli settlers in the same area build freely. Palestinian farmers are regularly denied access to their own agricultural land if it falls near settlement areas. Israeli settlements receive three to four times more water per capita than Palestinian communities in the same area.

The response to the apartheid designation: Israel and its supporters reject the term, arguing that Arab citizens of Israel proper have full civil rights (which is largely true, with significant documented inequalities), and that the West Bank situation is a temporary security arrangement pending a peace deal. The counterargument — made by the human rights reports — is that a 57-year "temporary" arrangement with no credible path to change, in which one population expands its presence and rights while the other loses land and freedoms, describes a permanent system regardless of what it is called.

The US Role — Why America Does This

Why does America fund all this? Over $260 billion to Israel — more than to any country in history, plus 50+ UN vetoes. Not sentiment. Strategic interest, oil, and a US arms industry that profits from every bomb.

The United States has provided Israel with more foreign aid than any other country in history — over $260 billion in total since 1948, in today's dollars. It provides $3.8 billion annually in military aid guaranteed by a 10-year memorandum of understanding. It has used its UN Security Council veto to block resolutions critical of Israel more than 50 times. It has supplied the weapons, intelligence, and diplomatic cover for every Israeli assault — including those that killed thousands of civilians.

This is not explained by sentiment or cultural affinity, though those exist. It is explained by the same logic that explains every other chapter of American foreign policy: strategic interest and the imperial mechanics described in the companion guide.

The strategic asset argument. In the Cold War framing, Israel served as a reliable pro-Western military power in a region of significant instability. It defeated Soviet-backed Arab states in 1967 and 1973, demonstrating the superiority of US military equipment. It shares intelligence with the US on regional threats. It hosts US military infrastructure. In return for this, it receives unconditional support — a transactional relationship dressed in the language of "shared values."

The oil politics. The Middle East contains the majority of the world's proven oil reserves. American strategic positioning in the region — including the relationship with Israel, the relationship with Saudi Arabia, and the wars in Iraq — is substantially about maintaining access to and pricing control over that resource. Israel's military dominance of the region is useful to this project.

The arms industry. US military aid to Israel is not simply a gift. It is largely required to be spent on US weapons systems — it is a subsidy to American arms manufacturers. Lockheed Martin, Boeing, Raytheon, and General Dynamics all profit directly from US military aid to Israel. When Israel uses US weapons to bomb civilian areas, it produces demand signals for more weapons and demonstrates their effectiveness in real conditions. This is the military-industrial logic the companion guide describes.

Deep Dive — The Israel Lobby

How US Policy Gets Shaped — and By Whom

US policy on Israel sits far to the right of US public opinion. A well-funded lobbying coalition — not a conspiracy — is a big part of why. Two mainstream scholars documented exactly how it works.

In 2006, political scientists John Mearsheimer and Stephen Walt — mainstream scholars at the University of Chicago and Harvard Kennedy School — published "The Israel Lobby and US Foreign Policy," first as an academic paper and then as a book. It created enormous controversy, but its core argument is empirically grounded.

The argument is not that there is a Jewish conspiracy. It is that a well-organised, well-funded domestic lobbying coalition — including organisations like AIPAC (the American Israel Public Affairs Committee), the Anti-Defamation League, and a network of evangelical Christian organisations — exerts disproportionate influence on US foreign policy toward Israel, beyond what US strategic interests alone would dictate.

AIPAC. AIPAC is one of the most powerful lobbying organisations in Washington. It does not make direct campaign contributions — but it effectively signals to donors which candidates support Israel's interests, and the money follows. Candidates who break with pro-Israel positions routinely face well-funded primary challenges. The result is a Congress where meaningful criticism of Israeli policy is politically dangerous regardless of party.

Evangelical support. A significant component of pro-Israel political support in the US comes not from Jewish Americans but from evangelical Christians, who hold theological views about Israel's role in end-times prophecy. This constituency forms a core part of the Republican base and has consistently pushed for unconditional US support of Israeli territorial expansion.

The limits of the lobby argument. It's important to be precise: the lobby argument does not explain everything. The US had strategic reasons to support Israel that predate the lobby's current influence. And the lobby doesn't cause the US to act against its interests — it shapes what policymakers understand their interests to be. The argument also risks sliding into antisemitic tropes if stated carelessly, which is why it requires precision: this is about a political lobbying operation, not about Jewish people as a group.

What the lobby means in practice: American politicians who might privately favour a more balanced approach to Palestine — conditioning aid, supporting UN resolutions, criticising settlement expansion — find it politically dangerous to say so publicly. The result is a US policy position far to the right of American public opinion, which polling consistently shows is more divided on Israel/Palestine than Congressional votes suggest.

Gaza Now — October 7 and After

On October 7, 2023, Hamas killed 1,139 people in Israel. In response, Israel launched one of the most destructive military campaigns of the 21st century — 37,000+ Palestinians killed, most of them women and children. Both facts are real. Here's how we got here.

On October 7, 2023, Hamas — the Islamist militant organisation that governs Gaza — launched a large-scale attack on southern Israel. Fighters crossed the fence that seals Gaza and attacked military bases, kibbutzim, and a music festival. 1,139 people were killed — the majority Israeli civilians, including children. Around 250 people were taken hostage into Gaza.

October 7 was the deadliest day for Jewish people since the Holocaust. That fact is real and cannot be minimised.

To understand October 7 without context is to misunderstand it completely. Gaza's 2.3 million people had been under comprehensive siege for 17 years. Israel had bombed them four times, killing thousands of civilians. They had no airport, heavily restricted import of goods including food and medicine, and no meaningful political horizon — the two-state solution was being steadily eliminated by settlements, and the Palestinian Authority in the West Bank had been rendered a US-backed security apparatus with no path to statehood. October 7 did not emerge from nowhere. It emerged from 75 years of dispossession and 17 years of siege. That context does not determine the moral evaluation of the attack on civilians. It is required to understand why it happened.

On resistance and atrocity: The killing of civilians — Israeli or Palestinian — is not the same as military resistance to occupation. Hamas's attack on the music festival and the kibbutzim killed civilians who were not combatants, including children. This requires acknowledgement. It also requires acknowledgement that Palestinian civilians in Gaza are not combatants, and that over 90% of the people Israel has killed in every assault on Gaza have been civilians. The violence of an occupied people resisting their occupation cannot be morally equated with the violence of the occupying state — but specific acts within a resistance movement that target civilians can and should be distinguished from the resistance itself.

The Israeli military response. Israel declared war on Gaza immediately after October 7. What followed is, by scale and speed, one of the most destructive military campaigns of the 21st century.

The last and most persistent myth: a solution is just around the corner — the "two-state solution" will fall into place any day now. The reality on the ground makes this a fantasy. The West Bank is the site of a massive, ongoing colonisation project. What the "two-state solution" actually offers Palestinians is a sad Bantustan with only about 20 percent sovereignty. The Gaza Strip is excluded from most discussions of a future state. Many parts of Jerusalem are not included in the envisaged state either.

The "two-state solution" is, in fact, an Israeli invention — designed to include the West Bank within Israel's control without incorporating its Palestinian population. "That part" of the West Bank would be nominally autonomous, perhaps even called a "state," in return for Palestinians giving up all their hopes: the return of refugees, equal rights for Palestinians in Israel, the fate of Jerusalem, and a normal life as human beings in their homeland.

Any criticism of this mythology is branded as anti-Semitism. But in fact, this policy and mythology are among the main reasons anti-Semitism persists. Israel insists that what it does, it does in the name of Judaism — creating an association between Zionist colonisation and the Jewish religion in the minds of twisted people. This association should be rejected in the name of Judaism itself.

For the sake of universal values, the rights of everyone who lives in Palestine — or was expelled from it — should be respected. The right for all peoples in Israel and Palestine to live as equals should top the agenda of all efforts for peace and reconciliation in the region.

The function of mythology: These ten myths are not innocent misunderstandings. They are the intellectual infrastructure of a settler-colonial project — each one designed to neutralise a specific moral objection. "The land was empty" neutralises the objection that someone already lived there. "They left voluntarily" neutralises the objection that they were expelled. "We offered peace" neutralises the objection that we took their land by force. "It's terrorism" neutralises the objection that they have a right to resist. Stack them together and they form a closed system: every fact that challenges the project is pre-answered by a myth that makes the fact disappear. That is why dismantling them matters — not as an academic exercise, but because a peace built on mythology is no peace at all.
